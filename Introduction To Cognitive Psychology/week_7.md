### Question 1

**According to the \_\_\_\_\_\_ view of concepts, people categorize new instances by comparing them to representations of previously stored instances.**

- A. Classical
- B. Prototype
- **C. Exemplar ✅**
- D. Schema

**Answer: C — Exemplar**

**Explanation:** The **exemplar view** of concepts holds that people do not form a single abstract prototype or a list of defining features. Instead, they store **actual specific instances (exemplars)** encountered in the past. When categorizing a new object, they **compare it to these stored exemplars** and assign it to the category whose exemplars it most closely resembles. This approach successfully explains why some category members feel more "typical" than others and why context can shift which exemplar comes to mind during categorization.

_(Ref: Galotti — Cognitive Psychology: In and Out of the Laboratory, Part III: Representation and Organization of Knowledge — Concepts and Categorization)_

### Question 2

**According to the typicality effect, the statement "A dog is a household pet" should be verified:**

- A. Faster than "a poodle is a household pet"
- B. Faster than "a dog is a living thing"
- **C. Faster than "a ferret is a household pet" ✅**
- D. Faster than "a dog is an animal"

**Answer: C — Faster than "a ferret is a household pet"**

**Explanation:** The **typicality effect** (Rosch, 1973) states that **typical category members are verified more quickly** than atypical ones. A *dog* is a highly typical household pet, whereas a *ferret* is a much less typical one. Therefore, "A dog is a household pet" is verified faster than "A ferret is a household pet." Option A is incorrect because *poodle* is a subordinate of *dog* (more specific), which can add processing time. Option D is incorrect because "animal" is a superordinate requiring upward hierarchical traversal.

_(Ref: Galotti — Cognitive Psychology: In and Out of the Laboratory, Part III: Representation and Organization of Knowledge — Concepts and Categorization)_

### Question 3

**Which of the following would be considered an example of a "natural kind" concept?**

- A. Blue
- **B. Wolf ✅**
- C. Odd number
- D. Mirror

**Answer: B — Wolf**

**Explanation:** **Natural kind concepts** refer to categories that exist independently in the natural world — biological species, substances, and natural phenomena — as opposed to human-made or abstract categories. A *wolf* is a naturally occurring biological species and is therefore a classic natural kind. *Blue* is a perceptual/color concept, *odd number* is a mathematical abstraction (a nominal kind), and *mirror* is an artifact (a human-made object) — none of these qualify as natural kinds.

_(Ref: Galotti — Cognitive Psychology: In and Out of the Laboratory, Part III: Representation and Organization of Knowledge — Concepts and Categorization)_

### Question 4

**A schema for a routine event, such as going to the dentist, is called a:**

- A. Concept
- **B. Script ✅**
- C. Exemplar
- D. Category

**Answer: B — Script**

**Explanation:** A **script** (Schank & Abelson, 1977) is a specific type of schema that represents the **typical sequence of events and actions** in a familiar, routine situation. Scripts organize knowledge about stereotyped event sequences — for example, the "going to the dentist" script would include: making an appointment → arriving at the clinic → sitting in the waiting room → being called in → undergoing treatment → paying → leaving. Scripts guide comprehension and allow people to make inferences about events that are not explicitly stated.

_(Ref: Galotti — Cognitive Psychology: In and Out of the Laboratory, Part III: Representation and Organization of Knowledge — Concepts and Categorization)_

### Question 5

**A \_\_\_\_\_\_\_ is a mental representation of some object, event, or pattern.**

- A. Category
- **B. Concept ✅**
- C. Script
- D. Memory

**Answer: B — Concept**

**Explanation:** A **concept** is defined as a **mental representation** of an object, event, idea, or pattern — the internal cognitive unit that stands for something in the world. Concepts allow us to group similar things together, make inferences, and communicate efficiently. A *category* is the external class of things that a concept represents; a *script* is a schema for event sequences; and *memory* is the broader cognitive system that stores and retrieves information — none of these are the direct mental representation itself.

_(Ref: Galotti — Cognitive Psychology: In and Out of the Laboratory, Part III: Representation and Organization of Knowledge — Concepts and Categorization)_

### Question 6

**A \_\_\_\_\_\_ can be defined as a class of similar things that share either an essential core, or some similarity in perceptual, biological, or functional properties.**

- **A. Category ✅**
- B. Concept
- C. Script
- D. Schema

**Answer: A — Category**

**Explanation:** A **category** is the **external grouping** — a class of entities that share an essential core or overlapping perceptual, biological, or functional properties. While a *concept* is the internal mental representation of such a class, the *category* is the class itself (the set of real-world members). *Scripts* are schemas for event sequences, and *schemas* are broader knowledge structures — neither refers specifically to a class defined by shared properties.

_(Ref: Galotti — Cognitive Psychology: In and Out of the Laboratory, Part III: Representation and Organization of Knowledge — Concepts and Categorization)_

### Question 7

**Which of the following is NOT TRUE of the classical view of concepts?**

- A. It proposes that concepts are mentally represented by a list of features.
- B. It assumes that membership in a category is clear-cut.
- **C. It accurately predicts the typicality effect. ✅**
- D. "Necessity" and "sufficient" features play an important role in the theory.

**Answer: C — It accurately predicts the typicality effect**

**Explanation:** The **classical view** (Aristotelian view) holds that categories are defined by **necessary and sufficient features**, and that category membership is **all-or-nothing** with clear-cut boundaries. This means that, under the classical view, all category members should be equally good members — there should be no **typicality gradient**. However, research consistently shows a strong **typicality effect** (some members are judged as better examples than others), which the classical view **cannot account for**. This failure is one of the major criticisms of the classical view.

_(Ref: Galotti — Cognitive Psychology: In and Out of the Laboratory, Part III: Representation and Organization of Knowledge — Concepts and Categorization)_

### Question 8

**Which of the following is a good example of a basic level of categorization?**

- A. Musical instrument
- **B. Piano ✅**
- C. Electronic keyboard
- D. String instrument

**Answer: B — Piano**

**Explanation:** Rosch's (1978) **basic level** is the most cognitively natural and efficient level of categorization — the level at which people most readily think, communicate, and interact with objects. In the musical instrument hierarchy: *Musical instrument* is **superordinate**, *Piano* is **basic level**, and *Electronic keyboard* is **subordinate** (more specific). *String instrument* is also a superordinate-like grouping. The basic level is preferred because it maximizes the ratio of within-category similarity to between-category similarity and is the level first acquired by children.

_(Ref: Galotti — Cognitive Psychology: In and Out of the Laboratory, Part III: Representation and Organization of Knowledge — Concepts and Categorization)_

### Question 9

**If "soda" is a basic-level category, then \_\_\_\_\_\_ would be a subordinate level.**

- A. Soft drink
- B. Beverage
- C. Drink
- **D. Coca-Cola ✅**

**Answer: D — Coca-Cola**

**Explanation:** In the three-tier hierarchy: *Beverage* / *Drink* / *Soft drink* are all broader, **superordinate** categories relative to *soda*, while **Coca-Cola** is a **specific brand/instance** — a more specific categorization below the basic level, making it the **subordinate** level. Subordinate-level categories provide more detail and specificity than the basic level; they are narrower groupings within the basic-level category.

_(Ref: Galotti — Cognitive Psychology: In and Out of the Laboratory, Part III: Representation and Organization of Knowledge — Concepts and Categorization)_

### Question 10

**The \_\_\_\_\_\_ view of concepts argues that a person uses his/her theories about the way the world works to justify the classification of instances in the same category.**

- A. Classical
- B. Prototype
- **C. Knowledge-based ✅**
- D. Schema

**Answer: C — Knowledge-based**

**Explanation:** The **knowledge-based view** (also called the **theory-based view**; Murphy & Medin, 1985) argues that categorization is not driven solely by feature similarity or prototype matching, but rather by a person's **underlying theories and causal knowledge** about the world. People group things into the same category when they have a coherent theoretical explanation for why those things belong together. For example, we classify a lead ball and a Styrofoam ball as both being "balls" not just because of feature overlap, but because we understand the concept of a ball in relation to how it functions in the world.

_(Ref: Galotti — Cognitive Psychology: In and Out of the Laboratory, Part III: Representation and Organization of Knowledge — Concepts and Categorization)_
