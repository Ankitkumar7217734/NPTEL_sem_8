### Question 1

**The school of psychology known as functionalism, which emphasized the purposes of the mind's operations, was founded by:**

- A. Wilhelm Wundt
- B. John Watson
- **C. William James ✅**
- D. Edward Titchener

**Answer: C — William James**

**Explanation:** Functionalism was founded by **William James**, who emphasized understanding mental processes in terms of their _adaptive purpose_ rather than their structure. Unlike structuralism, functionalism asked _why_ the mind operates the way it does, focusing on the functions and purposes of mental activity.

_(Ref: Chapter 1 – Cognitive Psychology: History, Methods, and Paradigms — Galotti)_

> ⚠️ **Note on Answer Key:** The provided answer key lists "A," but the correct answer is **C (William James)**. Wilhelm Wundt (A) founded **structuralism**, not functionalism.

### Question 2

**The fact that you know you have seen the face before but cannot remember the name of the person, illustrates the cognitive process of:**

- A. Perception
- B. Attention
- **C. Recognition ✅**
- D. Recall

**Answer: C — Recognition**

**Explanation:** **Recognition** involves identifying previously encountered information without necessarily retrieving full contextual details. In this case, familiarity with the face is triggered, but the name — a contextually associated detail — cannot be retrieved. This is distinct from **recall**, which requires actively retrieving information from memory without a present cue.

_(Ref: Chapter 1 – Cognitive Psychology: History, Methods, and Paradigms — Galotti)_

### Question 3

**The term \*\*\_\*\* refers to the relevance of the research to the "real world."**

- A. Internal validity
- B. Evolutionary validity
- **C. Ecological validity ✅**
- D. Neural validity

**Answer: C — Ecological Validity**

**Explanation:** **Ecological validity** refers to the degree to which research findings can be generalized to real-world settings outside of the laboratory. High ecological validity means the study's conditions closely mirror natural, everyday situations. This concept is especially important in cognitive psychology, where lab tasks may not always reflect how cognition works in daily life.

_(Ref: Chapter 1 – Paradigms of Cognitive Psychology (Ecological Approach) — Galotti)_

### Question 4

**\_\_\_ challenged the assumption that mental events were beyond the realm of scientific study.**

- **A. Cognitive revolution ✅**
- B. Behaviorist rebellion
- C. Human factor movements
- D. Universal grammar

**Answer: A — Cognitive Revolution**

**Explanation:** The **cognitive revolution** (circa 1950s–1960s) directly challenged behaviorism's dismissal of mental events. It re-established the legitimacy of studying internal mental processes — such as memory, attention, and problem-solving — using scientific methods. Key contributors included Noam Chomsky, George Miller, and Ulric Neisser.

_(Ref: Chapter 1 – The Cognitive Revolution — Galotti)_

### Question 5

**Since the 1970s, various techniques of \*\*\_\*\* have allowed us to construct pictures of the anatomy and functioning of intact brains.**

- A. Genetic engineering
- **B. Brain imaging ✅**
- C. Neurosurgery
- D. Brain lesioning

**Answer: B — Brain Imaging**

**Explanation:** **Brain imaging techniques** developed since the 1970s — including CT scans, PET scans, fMRI, and EEG — allow researchers to examine both the structure and functioning of the intact human brain _non-invasively_. These tools have been transformative for cognitive neuroscience and cognitive psychology.

_(Ref: Chapter 1 – Cognitive Psychology: History, Methods, and Paradigms — Galotti)_

### Question 6

**Historians date the founding of scientific psychology to the 1879 laboratory of:**

- A. William James
- **B. Wilhelm Wundt ✅**
- C. John Locke
- D. Edward Titchener

**Answer: B — Wilhelm Wundt**

**Explanation:** **Wilhelm Wundt** established the first formal experimental psychology laboratory in Leipzig, Germany in **1879**, marking the birth of psychology as a distinct scientific discipline. Wundt's approach, known as **structuralism**, used introspection to analyze the basic elements of conscious experience.

_(Ref: Chapter 1 – Structuralism — Galotti)_

### Question 7

**The term "limited capacity processors" suggests that:**

- A. Humans have limited memory storage
- **B. Human beings can only do so many things at once ✅**
- C. Neurons can only fire at a certain rate
- D. Processing in the brain occurs in parallel

**Answer: B — Human beings can only do so many things at once**

**Explanation:** The concept of **limited-capacity processors** in cognitive psychology means that human cognitive resources — including attention, working memory, and processing — are finite. This limits how many tasks or how much information can be handled simultaneously. This principle is central to the **information-processing approach** to cognition.

_(Ref: Chapter 1 – Information-Processing Approach — Galotti)_

### Question 8

**This psychologist sought to describe the intellectual structures underlying cognitive experience at different developmental points through an approach he called genetic epistemology:**

- A. Galton
- B. Skinner
- C. Wundt
- **D. Piaget ✅**

**Answer: D — Piaget**

**Explanation:** **Jean Piaget** developed **genetic epistemology** — a framework to explain how knowledge and cognitive structures develop across different stages of childhood (sensorimotor, preoperational, concrete operational, and formal operational). His work was foundational to developmental and cognitive psychology.

_(Ref: Chapter 1 – Genetic Epistemology — Galotti)_

### Question 9

**Clinical interviews are to introspection as \_\_ are to \_\_\_\_.**

- A. Naturalistic observations; experiments
- B. Naturalistic observations; quasi experiments
- **C. Controlled observations; naturalistic observations ✅**
- D. Controlled observations; experiments

**Answer: C — Controlled observations; naturalistic observations**

**Explanation:** This is an **analogy question**. Clinical interviews are a _structured/controlled_ version of introspection, just as **controlled observations** are a more structured version of **naturalistic observations**. The analogy highlights the relationship between less controlled, more naturalistic methods and their structured counterparts.

_(Ref: Chapter 1 – Research Methods in Cognitive Psychology — Galotti)_

### Question 10

**Which of the following is a basic assumption of the connectionist approach?**

- A. Serial processing
- B. Multiple stores where information is kept throughout processing
- **C. Networks of connections among simple processing units ✅**
- D. A central processor that directs the flow of information

**Answer: C — Networks of connections among simple processing units**

**Explanation:** The **connectionist (or parallel distributed processing) approach** assumes that cognition emerges from vast **networks of interconnected simple units** that process information simultaneously. This differs from the classical information-processing model, which assumes a central processor and serial processing. Connectionism draws inspiration from the neural architecture of the brain.

_(Ref: Chapter 1 – Connectionist Approach — Galotti)_
