Question 1

According to the notion of cognitive economy, a characteristic like "has wings" would be stored along with which of the following semantic memory nodes.

  • A. Bird ✅
  • B. Ostrich
  • C. Hummingbird
  • D. All of the above

Answer: A — Bird

Explanation: According to cognitive economy, properties shared by an entire category are stored at the highest applicable level in the conceptual hierarchy rather than being redundantly repeated at every subordinate node. Since "has wings" is a property common to virtually all birds, it is stored at the Bird node — not repeated at subordinate nodes like Ostrich or Hummingbird. This principle was central to Collins & Quillian's (1969) hierarchical network model of semantic memory.

(Ref: Galotti — Cognitive Psychology: In and Out of the Laboratory, Part II: Basic Cognitive Processes — Memory Processes: Semantic Memory)

Question 2

Priming in lexical decision tasks may be explained by the idea of:

  • A. Episodic memory
  • B. Encoding specificity
  • C. Spreading activation ✅
  • D. Typicality effects

Answer: C — Spreading activation

Explanation: In a lexical decision task, participants decide whether a letter string is a real word. Priming occurs when a preceding related word (the prime) speeds up responses to a target word. This is best explained by spreading activation (Collins & Loftus, 1975): activation from the prime node spreads through the semantic network to related nodes, pre-activating them and lowering the threshold needed for recognition. For example, "bread" primes "butter" because the two nodes are closely linked in the network.

(Ref: Galotti — Cognitive Psychology: In and Out of the Laboratory, Part II: Basic Cognitive Processes — Memory Processes: Semantic Memory)

Question 3

Which of the following is a good example of a superordinate level of categorization?

  • A. Fruit ✅
  • B. Banana
  • C. Fuji apple
  • D. Golden delicious apple

Answer: A — Fruit

Explanation: Rosch's (1978) basic-level theory identifies three levels in the conceptual hierarchy: superordinate (most general), basic, and subordinate (most specific). In the domain of food: Fruit is the superordinate level, Banana or Apple is the basic level, and Fuji apple or Golden Delicious apple is the subordinate level. Superordinate categories are the broadest and share the fewest features across members.

(Ref: Galotti — Cognitive Psychology: In and Out of the Laboratory, Part II: Basic Cognitive Processes — Memory Processes: Semantic Memory)

Question 4

The schema view of concept formation assumes that:

  • A. There are clear boundaries among individual schemata
  • B. There is cognitive economy among concepts
  • C. Information is abstracted across instances ✅
  • D. No information is stored about actual instances

Answer: C — Information is abstracted across instances

Explanation: The schema view of concept formation holds that people extract and store abstract, generalized representations formed across many individual encounters with a category. Rather than storing a perfect prototype or every individual exemplar, schemata capture the common structure, typical attributes, and typical relationships found across instances. Note that schemata do not require perfectly clear boundaries (option A) and do not claim that no instance-specific information is ever retained (option D).

(Ref: Galotti — Cognitive Psychology: In and Out of the Laboratory, Part II: Basic Cognitive Processes — Memory Processes: Semantic Memory)

Question 5

According to research by Collins and Quillian, the statement "Siamese cats have blue eyes" will be verified:

  • A. Slower than "Siamese cats give birth to live young"
  • B. Faster than "Siamese cats give birth to live young" ✅
  • C. In the same amount of time as "Siamese cats give birth to live young"
  • D. Slower than "Siamese cats have tails"

Answer: B — Faster than "Siamese cats give birth to live young"

Explanation: Collins & Quillian's hierarchical network model predicts that verification time increases with the number of hierarchical levels that must be traversed. "Siamese cats have blue eyes" is a subordinate-level property stored directly at the Siamese cat node, requiring minimal traversal. By contrast, "Siamese cats give birth to live young" is a mammal-level property, requiring the system to travel up through Cat → Mammal, taking longer. Therefore, the blue-eyes statement is verified faster.

(Ref: Galotti — Cognitive Psychology: In and Out of the Laboratory, Part II: Basic Cognitive Processes — Memory Processes: Semantic Memory)

Question 6

Conrad has found evidence that the statement "A shark can move" can be verified in the same amount of time as "An animal can move." These results suggest that reaction time is best predicted by:

  • A. Cognitive economy
  • B. Frequency of associations ✅
  • C. Encoding specificity
  • D. Episodic memory

Answer: B — Frequency of associations

Explanation: Collins & Quillian's hierarchical model predicts that "A shark can move" (two levels up) should be verified slower than "An animal can move" (zero levels for Animal). Conrad's finding that both statements are verified equally fast challenges the strict hierarchical distance account. Instead, it supports the idea that associative strength (frequency of association) — how often "shark" and "move" co-occur in experience — is a better predictor of reaction time than hierarchical distance alone. This was a key insight that motivated Collins & Loftus's later spreading activation model.

(Ref: Galotti — Cognitive Psychology: In and Out of the Laboratory, Part II: Basic Cognitive Processes — Memory Processes: Semantic Memory)

Question 7

Which of the following would be a part of your declarative memory system?

  • A. Knowing how to ride a bicycle
  • B. Knowing how to drive a car
  • C. Knowing how to react at a red light
  • D. Being able to name a hybrid car ✅

Answer: D — Being able to name a hybrid car

Explanation: Declarative memory (also called explicit memory) encompasses knowledge that can be consciously recalled and verbally stated, including both episodic (personal events) and semantic (general factual knowledge) memory. Being able to name a hybrid car is accessing a semantic fact — a classic example of declarative knowledge. The other options (riding a bicycle, driving a car, reacting at a red light) all involve procedural memory, a form of non-declarative (implicit) memory that encodes motor skills and habits not easily verbalized.

(Ref: Galotti — Cognitive Psychology: In and Out of the Laboratory, Part II: Basic Cognitive Processes — Memory Processes: Semantic Memory)

Question 8

Collins and Loftus's spreading activation theory differs from the hierarchical network theory in that:

  • A. It dispenses with the idea of cognitive economy ✅
  • B. It relies on the assumption of hierarchical structure
  • C. It cannot account for the typicality effect
  • D. It makes stronger predictions than hierarchical models

Answer: A — It dispenses with the idea of cognitive economy

Explanation: Collins & Quillian's original hierarchical model relied on cognitive economy — properties are stored at the highest applicable level to avoid redundancy. Collins & Loftus (1975) revised this in their spreading activation model: concepts are organized by semantic relatedness (not rigid hierarchy), and properties can be stored at multiple nodes regardless of hierarchical level. This eliminates strict cognitive economy. The spreading activation model also better accounts for typicality effects (option C), associative strength, and other phenomena that the hierarchical model failed to explain.

(Ref: Galotti — Cognitive Psychology: In and Out of the Laboratory, Part II: Basic Cognitive Processes — Memory Processes: Semantic Memory)

Question 9

"Characteristic features" and "family resemblance" are important aspects of the _________ view of concepts.

  • A. Classical
  • B. Prototype ✅
  • C. Exemplar
  • D. Schema

Answer: B — Prototype

Explanation: The prototype view (Rosch, 1973) holds that people represent categories as a prototype — an idealized, average representation capturing the most characteristic features of category members. Members are judged as category instances based on their resemblance to this prototype. Family resemblance (Wittgenstein) captures the idea that members share overlapping features without any single feature being necessary or sufficient — exactly the logic underlying the prototype view. The classical view requires necessary and sufficient features (clear boundaries), while exemplar views store actual encountered instances, and schema views emphasize abstracted relational structures.

(Ref: Galotti — Cognitive Psychology: In and Out of the Laboratory, Part II: Basic Cognitive Processes — Memory Processes: Semantic Memory)

Question 10

Studies of semantic memory have shown that in a lexical decision task, people are faster at responding to the stimulus "bread" if it is paired with a stimulus such as:

  • A. "rencle"
  • B. "dog"
  • C. "island"
  • D. "butter" ✅

Answer: D — "butter"

Explanation: This is a classic demonstration of semantic priming via spreading activation. When the word "bread" is processed, activation spreads through the semantic network to closely associated nodes — and "butter" is one of the strongest associates of "bread" (they frequently co-occur and are semantically/functionally related). This pre-activation lowers the threshold for recognizing "butter" as a real word, resulting in a faster lexical decision. The other options ("rencle" is a non-word, "dog" and "island" are semantically unrelated) would produce no such priming benefit.

(Ref: Galotti — Cognitive Psychology: In and Out of the Laboratory, Part II: Basic Cognitive Processes — Memory Processes: Semantic Memory)
